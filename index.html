New York Times Headlines 1991-2020

Welcome to Our Project

Our world is reflected and influenced by the reporting of major media companies, which observe and spread news stories about events in a digestible and meaningful way. The tecnological advancements of the last several decades have also shown us that media is not only a game of reporting, but also capturing readers' attention. Thus, headlines of news articles seek to toe the line between delivering information with journalistic integrity and also piquing peoples' curiousity. We sought to answer the following questions or achieve the following goals with the New York Times Headlines from the years 1991 to 2020.

1. What was important to readers over the years 1991-2020. How is it different to what is important to readers now?

2. How can we piece together historical events and stories through the headlines of the New York Times?

3. This website visualization serves as the exploratory data analysis component of our CS1090A class, a class on data science. Once we are done with that project, we will provide the link to those materials as well. We worked on principle component analysis of our data and also built predictive models for regression and classification.

Observe the next two scatter plots below with different colored data points. Principle Component Analysis allows us to see that semantically, there are differences between different headlines in the "business, economy, and finance" and "travel" news desks. We also see that there are differences between the "Media" and "Sports" news desks. This is because the different colored data points tend to bunch up into clusters with other data points of the same color. A little more about this below...

Part of our project involved performing principle component analysis (PCA) on the vector embeddings of the headlines. We were able to use a sentence transformer Python library that converts text into very high dimensional vectors. Then, these vectors can be contained in one data matrix, which means that we can perform principle component analysis on it! We won't discuss to much of what PCA actually is mathematically. If you are unfamiliar, just know that it can used to group complicated data into clusters and look for patterns.

As we can see, the vector embeddings of headlines from different news desks actually do form different clusters, which means that there do exist semantic differences in the headlines of different news desks that Python's sentence transformer library can detect. Since the contents of the headlines are different, they will receive different values in their components of the vectors, and PCA does the best job of visualizing these complicated differences.

Next, we combed through keywords in the headlines that jumped out at us throughout the years. It was very cool and surprising in some cases to see what historical events can and cannot be observed from looking at an overview of the headlines. This project has taught us that media is an everchanging landscape. Even within the record keeping and data of these esteemed media companies like the New York Times, the data formatting is inconsistent and disorganized at times. So for the average reader, it definitely will be difficult to keep track of how headlines change over time.

PCA still works when we include many different news desks categories. If anything, it is even more interesting now. Interpretting the different clusters that form above, we see that all the business, economy, and finance related headlines tend to be projected into the bottom area of the scatter plot, signifying that these points are kind of in a region of their own in the high dimensional vector space.

Then we see that the world, international, and foreign news desk seems to form a cluster on the left, while the weekend news forms a cluster on the right. Finally, the home news desk forms a cluster in between the "world, international, and foreign" and "weekend" clusters. Does this PCA visualization make sense intuitively?

Through the years, what events can we observe and learn about just from looking at the headlines.

1991

In 1991, the Soviet Union officially dissolved amidst great political instability and calls for reform in its eastern satellite states. Mikhail Gorbachev, the former president of the Soviet Union, attempted to improve government transparency and civil liberties like freedom of speech and press through his policies of Glasnost. He even attempted introducing market reforms to the Soviet economy through his policies of Perestroika.

What was the New York Times' coverage of these reforms?

In the 1991 opinion section article (author not found in our dataset), titled "Broken Glasnost", heavily criticized the Glasnost policies as a failing to lead up to their ideological promises of openness, "Now, instead of openness, Orwellian propaganda spews forth". Similar sentiments are echoed in George Whitney's 1991 article "Moscow's Iron Hand: Gorbachev, Facing Growing Disorder, Chooses Control over Liberalization".

In the 11 thousand headlines that we have ChatGPT's y-values for, 32 headlines contain the words "soviet", "russian", or "ussr". The average catestrophe-to-miracle sentiment score of those 32 headlines was around -0.06. This is slightly lower than America's score of approximately -0.01.

Amidst great political and economic instability The Russian oil industry suffered heavy hits. Russian involvement in the Gulf War also stretched its resources thin. That same year, the 1991 Racha 7.2 Magnitude Earthquake struck the region of modern day Georgia, killing around 100 civilians. All of these events contributed to the fall of the Soviet Union, and the New York Times covered all these events closely. The word 'Soviet' is included 566 times in our database in the year 1991, one of the most commonly occuring keywords that year.

2001

Surprisingly, simply filtering through headlines, it is difficult to find a lot of articles about the attacks on the New York twin towers on September 11th, 2001, especially considering this event took place in New York City.

2007

As many Americans are aware, throughout 2007 and 2008, the United States and experienced a large scale financial crisis, paralleling the 1929 Wall Street crash that started the Great Depression. Interestingly enough, this period of time also marks a change in some of the coverage of the New York Times between different news desks.

As you may have already observed in the line graph above, during this time, the number of articles published in the business, economy, and finance news desk stayed high, before dropping after the financial crisis was relieved in the following years from 2010 to 2012. As for other news desks, this period marked the peak in the coverage of the world, entertainment and entertainment news desks. It marked stark dropoffs in the sports, home, weekend and media news desks.

Some of this change could just be the New York Times moving similar content to another news desks under a new name. Perhaps it could show pivoting focuses in the New York Times coverage. What do you think?

During this time, we start seeing articles covering the financial crisis itself with headlines like "Following the Financial Crisis and the Markets" and "Fannie Mae and the Financial Crisis" . Many other articles cover the effect of the crisis on certain other regions and countries, like a "Financial Crises Spread in Europe" or "In Japan, Financial Crisis Is Just a Ripple" . Some articles also cover the effect on certain industries or institutions, like the 2008 "Idea of the Day" blog post "Education and the Financial Crisis" .

These are some of the major headlines concerning the stock market and financial crisis published around that time period, through the years 2007 to 2010. We filtered for headlines that contained the word "recession".

2016

When we search the top keywords in the headlines of most years, the name of the president at the time usually shows up. Like Clinton and Bush. 2016 marked a big change in this trend. In our analysis of the headlines, in the 2016 election, the names of multiple candidates and the president at the time all show up as top keywords in the headlines.
